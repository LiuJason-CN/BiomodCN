What is the use of a new-born baby?


The design and assembly of DNA catenanes is a new subfield, besides of DNA self-assembly and DNA origami, in DNA nanotechnology, and adds another recognition layer on top of the Waston-Crick design rules. Our design principle for Triad provides a new idea and lays foundation to the assembly of DNA catenanes. It is perspective to know the precious values and bright future of DNA catenanes, not only be aesthetically appealing but also could be suitable components for the construction of molecular motors or biosensors made from DNA. Beyond that, it is reasonable to reckon that more and more applications will come after. One summer is not enough for us to exploit further application of catenane we made, but some potential ideas have been conceived for its application. Here, we want to share these ideas and it would be of great pleasure to see some of them are accomplished in the future.

Immunostimulation enhancer

DNA containing unmethylated CpG dinucleotides (CpG DNA) is a potential activator of innate and acquired immune responses. Previous report1 has shown that Y-shaped oligodeoxynucleotides (Y-ODN) containing CpG sequence could induce more intense immunostimulation than double-stranded ODN (dsODN). However, the short half-time of Y-ODN in serum impeded its widespread use. One possible explanation for this phenomenon is that Y-ODN contains three sticky ends which are susceptible to nuclease-mediated degradation. The Triad we made is a special form of Y-ODN, which there is no sticky end in. This degradation can be avoided by taking advantages of our Triad. Hence, Triad is a good candidate as an immunostimulation enhancer. Using elaborate design, dsDNA Triad which contains multi-CpG sequence motifs could be developed.Because of its unique property, we believe DNA triad could shed light on adjuvant and immunotherapy.

 

Chain reaction carrier

As we know, chain reactions, such as fatty acid synthesis, are common in nature. They are not only important in our biological activities, but also useful in industrial manufacture. However, engineering those reactions is difficult and tedious, since it is hard to manage so many reactions within one system. DNA catenanes may throw light on this field. Our idea is illustrated in Figure 1. Rings on DNA catenane are used as enzyme carriers in a chain reaction. By catenation, enzymes can be recruited and be released in order according to the reaction procedure. Once ingredients are added, reactions will be started with high efficiency and great controllability. Compared to conventional method, DNA catenane is easy to fabricate and control in multi-step reactions, which may open a new route to chemical engineering.



Figure 1. Illustration of Chain reaction carrier.

 

New biosensors

The rings in DNA catenane are not only connected but also independent, which makes it possible to carry out many independent functions within one molecule. Combining DNA recognition elements (such as aptamer binding) and signal amplification (like rolling cycle amplification, RCA) together, we can develop new biosensors for the detection of various targets, i.e. protein.

Herein, we demonstrate a brief design of a two-ring catenane biosensor used for protein detection.



Figure 2. A biosensor based on DNA catenane.

As shown in Figure 2, the aptamer sequence is inserted in one ring (red, ring 1) and RCA primer sequence is designed in the other (green, ring 2). The aptamer sequence is partly hybridized with RCA primer binding sequence to block the RCA primer. Once aptamer binds with its target protein, it will dissociate from the RCA primer and expose the RCA primer region. The exposed RCA primer could anneal to DNA template on ring 2, and trigger a RCA reaction when polymerase and dNTP are added. The RCA products are then monitored by methods like molecular beacons2, fluorophores3, or molecular zippers4. In short, the protein signal is conversed to DNA signal with a high amplification. The convenience and cheapness of this method may open a new route to biosensor.
